PART 1: HOW HR SCREENING ACTUALLY WORKS

HR筛选的真实运作方式 — 理解游戏规则才能赢

The Game: HR has a structured scorecard (checklist). Each item is scored Pass / Concern / Fail. They need enough "Pass" marks to forward you to the hiring manager. HR does NOT evaluate your technical depth — they check signals. Your job is to give them clear, checkable signals on every item.
Milly's Role: She is a recruiter, not an engineer. She will:
• Follow a script of 8-12 questions in a fixed order
• Type notes into an ATS (Applicant Tracking System) while you talk
• Score each answer against pre-defined criteria from the hiring manager
• Decide in the first 5 minutes if you're a "yes" or "maybe" — the rest confirms
• Write a summary paragraph for the hiring manager within 1 hour after the call
What She Reports to the Hiring Manager:
• Communication quality (clear? structured? confident?)
• Motivation level (why this role? why Nebius?)
• Experience match (years, tools, domain)
• Red flags (gaps, job-hopping, salary mismatch, visa issues)
• Availability & logistics (start date, location, work authorization)
• Overall recommendation: Strong Yes / Yes / Maybe / No

PART 6: SALARY NEGOTIATION — THE CHESS GAME

薪资谈判 — 棋局策略

HR's Goal: Determine if you're within budget BEFORE investing more interview time. If your number is way above budget, they'll reject you now to save everyone's time. If it's way below, they'll note it as a positive (cheap hire).

Your Goal: Stay in the game without anchoring too low. The real negotiation happens AFTER the offer, not now.
The 3-Step Salary Dance:

Step 1 — Deflect: "I'd love to learn more about the role and the team before discussing specific numbers. Could you share the budget range for this position?"

Step 2 — If she insists: "Based on my research of data engineering roles in Amsterdam, I'm looking at a total compensation in the range of 90 to 110 thousand euros. But I'm flexible — the role and growth opportunity matter more to me than the exact number."

Step 3 — If she gives a range: "That sounds reasonable. I'm sure we can find something that works for both sides."
NEVER DO:
• Never give a single number (e.g., "I want 100K"). Always give a range.
• Never say your current salary is zero (even though you're not employed). Say "I prefer to focus on the market rate for this role."
• Never ask about salary first. Let her bring it up.
• Never say "I'll take anything" — this signals desperation.

Every risk should be reframed as a strength. The candidate who makes HR's job easiest (clear answers, no red flags, obvious enthusiasm) is the one who moves forward.
