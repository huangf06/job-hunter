## Conversation Approach

### What NOT to do
- Don't treat it as a job interview
- Don't pitch yourself or sell your skills
- Don't bring up the rejection defensively
- Don't over-prepare scripted answers
- Don't ask "are there any open positions?"

### What TO do
- Be genuinely curious about ENPICOM and what they build
- Ask about the engineering challenges (they process trillions of reads!)
- Share your own engineering interests honestly
- Be the same person who wrote that self-aware email
- Let him see how you think, naturally

### Opening
Keep it simple. Thank him for making time. He's a direct, informal person.
"Thanks for making this happen, Jorrit. Really looking forward to hearing about what you're building."

## Talking Points (Natural Conversation, Not a Script)

### 1. His Journey (Show Genuine Interest)
He went from graphic design → medical informatics → bioinformatics CTO. That's fascinating.
- "I saw you studied photography alongside CS at Twente -- that's an unusual combination. Does that background still influence how you think about product design?"
- "Going from AMC medical imaging research to building the IGX Platform -- how did that transition happen?"

### 2. ENPICOM's Tech Challenges (Engineer-to-Engineer)
These are legitimately interesting problems. Show you've done homework:
- "Processing 1.55M+ reads per minute at petabyte scale -- what does that architecture actually look like day-to-day? Are there bottlenecks your team is actively working on?"
- "I saw the engineering blog post about migrating from GCP to AWS. You described EKS as 'sold without batteries' -- did that assessment hold up over time?"
- "The move to Redshift for your data warehouse layer -- is that serving you well for the query patterns scientists need?"

### 3. Your Honest Story (If It Comes Up Naturally)
If he asks about your background:
- Be honest about the ML/data identity vs. SWE
- You can mention your lakehouse project shows you can build production data systems
- The job-hunter project (which he already saw) demonstrates real engineering: multi-stage pipeline, Playwright scraping, Claude API integration, Jinja2 rendering, SQLite backend
- **Don't oversell**: He already knows your profile doesn't match their current need

### 4. The Job-Hunter Project (He'll Probably Bring It Up)
He already found it and commented on it. He might want to discuss it:
- Be honest about what you learned from his feedback
- You can share what changed in your approach since then
- The project is genuinely interesting engineering -- own it without apologizing

### 5. Common Ground
- **VU Amsterdam**: You both have VU connections (CEO Nicola Bonzanni's PhD is from VU, you did your MSc AI there)
- **Data-intensive systems**: His publication was about compression for medical imaging; you work with data pipelines
- **Startups/small teams**: He was at ENPICOM when it was tiny; you were first data hire at GLP

### 6. Intelligent Questions to Ask
- "With ~30 people and 84% in R&D, how do you balance engineering depth with product breadth?"
- "You have a Chief AI Officer now -- how is AI/ML integrated into the platform versus the core software engineering?"
- "What's the hardest hiring challenge for ENPICOM right now?"
- "How do you think about the build-vs-buy decision for bioinformatics tools?"

## Red Lines (Things to Avoid)

1. **Don't ask directly about job openings** -- if he has something in mind, he'll bring it up
2. **Don't badmouth AI/LLMs** -- be nuanced; you use them, he has opinions about them
3. **Don't exaggerate your TypeScript/React skills** -- he knows you don't have them
4. **Don't promise to learn his stack "quickly"** -- that's interview talk, not coffee chat talk
5. **Don't stay too long** -- 60-90 minutes is ideal. He booked 2 hours but that's buffer
