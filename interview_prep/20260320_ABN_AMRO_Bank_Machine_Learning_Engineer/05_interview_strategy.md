Do not bluff.

Recommended answer:

> My strongest hands-on depth is in Python, Spark-adjacent data systems, Databricks, orchestration, and ML lifecycle thinking. My direct Azure production depth is lighter. MLflow is something I know through certification and adjacent work rather than years of deep ownership in a large production platform. I would describe that as a real gap, but also a learnable one, because the surrounding engineering patterns are already familiar to me.

### "Why should we believe you can ramp fast in our environment?"

Recommended answer shape:

1. you have already switched domains before
2. you learn best by owning real systems
3. your pattern is to reduce ambiguity through structure, tests, and documentation
4. you are explicit about gaps early instead of hiding them

Suggested framing:

> The reason I think I can ramp fast is that I have done this kind of transition before: new domain, high expectations, unclear systems, and the need to create structure quickly. My way of learning is practical. I map the workflow, clarify the failure modes, identify the non-negotiable quality gates, and contribute in the parts where my existing strengths already transfer.

### "How do you work with data scientists?"

Good structure:

1. understand their workflow and pain points
2. reduce manual friction
3. standardize where reliability matters
4. avoid imposing platform complexity for its own sake
5. agree early on expectations for quality, deployment, and monitoring

### "How would you productionalize an ML model?"

Use this shape:

1. define business objective and failure modes
2. pin down data contracts and validation
3. make training reproducible
4. track model and data versions
5. test inference and deployment paths
6. monitor model performance, data quality, and drift
7. define rollback / retraining / ownership processes

- claiming deep Azure expertise you do not have
- sounding like a research-only candidate
- using generic MLOps buzzwords without real examples
- ignoring the compliance and governance side of bank ML
- talking only about models, not engineering discipline
